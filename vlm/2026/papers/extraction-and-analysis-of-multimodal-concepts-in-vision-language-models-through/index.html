    Extraction and Analysis of Multimodal Concepts in Vision Language Models through Sparse Autoencoders¶

    🕒 Published (v1): 2026-06-19 08:08 UTC · Source: Arxiv · link

    Why this paper was selected

    Sparse autoencoders reveal multimodal concepts inside VLMs; mechanistic interpretability angle

    TL;DR¶

    This paper presents a Sparse Autoencoder (SAE)-based framework that jointly extracts visual, textual, and multimodal concepts from VLMs—something prior SAE work ignored. By augmenting masked-image queries with complementary text (and vice versa), the method improves visual concept quality by up to 45% over the prior SAE baseline while maintaining comparable textual concept quality and enabling systematic identification of multimodal neurons.

    Problem¶

    Existing SAE-based mechanistic interpretability work extracts either textual or visual concepts in isolation, with no systematic treatment of multimodal concepts—those jointly grounded in both image and language tokens. Additionally, prior visual extraction approaches mask out non-activating image patches without contextual text, producing vague, low-quality concept descriptions that poorly align with the input images. This prevents a complete understanding of how VLMs reason across modalities.

    Method¶

    The framework operates on a VLM (LLaVA-NeXT-8B-HF) augmented with a pretrained SAE (\(\omega = 2^{17}\) neurons, \(k=256\) TopK sparsity, trained on layer 24). For each VQA sample \(j\) containing image patches and text tokens, per-neuron average activations are computed separately: $\(\bar{V}^{(j)} = \frac{1}{P}\sum_{i=1}^P v_i^{(j)}, \quad \bar{T}^{(j)} = \frac{1}{N_j}\sum_{i=1}^{N_j} t_i^{(j)}\)$ For each neuron, the top-5 activating samples are selected per modality. Visual concept extraction feeds the masked image (non-activating patches blacked out) plus the corresponding Q&A text to LLaVA-72B as explainer. Textual concept extraction highlights activating tokens in brackets plus the unmodified image. Each neuron thus receives up to two hypotheses (\(h_\text{vis}\), \(h_\text{text}\)). Neurons with both hypotheses are labeled multimodal if the CLIP ViT-B/32 and ALIGN cosine similarity between the two text encodings both exceed \(\tau = 0.8\); otherwise, the modality with higher hypothesis–data alignment wins. Evaluation uses two settings: direct hypothesis–data alignment (CLIP/ALIGN similarity with blurred-inactive-patch images or token sequences) and category-based alignment against six Network Dissection labels.

    Key Contributions¶

    • Unified multimodal extraction framework: first SAE-based method to jointly extract visual, textual, and multimodal concepts from a VLM in a single pipeline.
    • Improved visual extraction via cross-modal grounding: adding the Q&A text alongside masked images during explainer queries yields up to 45% higher CLIP/ALIGN hypothesis–data alignment vs. the image-only masked baseline.
    • Multimodal neuron classification: threshold-based scheme (\(\tau = 0.8\) on CLIP+ALIGN text-encoder cosine similarity) assigns each neuron to visual, textual, or multimodal; sensitivity to \(\tau\) is reported.
    • Qualitative intervention study: forced neuron activation (\(\alpha = 60\), \(\approx20\times\) average) shows multimodal concepts exert stronger influence on text generation than unimodal ones.

    Results¶

    • Visual concept alignment (hypothesis–data): at threshold \(n=1\), method achieves CLIP avg 0.45 vs. baseline 0.15 (+200%); ALIGN avg 0.47 vs. 0.13. Averaged across \(n \in \{1,\ldots,5\}\): CLIP 0.658 vs. 0.246 (+41%), ALIGN 0.670 vs. 0.220 (+45%).
    • Category-based visual alignment (6 Network Dissection labels): method 21.98% vs. baseline 20.84% (CLIP); near-parity under ALIGN (16.74% vs. 16.72%), confirming the gain is not at the cost of categorical precision.
    • Textual concept quality (detection/fuzzing scores on LLaVA-NeXT sentences): LLaVA-based extractor scores 0.78/0.80, comparable to Llama-3.1-8B (0.81/0.83) and Gemma-2-9B (0.76/0.74)—no regression from adding visual context.
    • Concept counts (first 5000 SAE neurons): method finds 2291 textual, 470 visual, 132 multimodal concepts; baseline finds 1391 visual only (no textual or multimodal).
    • Multimodal neuron count sensitivity: 223 at \(\tau=0.70\), 132 at \(\tau=0.80\), 23 at \(\tau=0.90\).

    Limitations¶

    • Evaluation on a single SAE architecture (one layer, one VLM); generalizability across layers, model families, or tasks is untested.
    • Concept hypotheses are constrained to short descriptions to suit CLIP/ALIGN scoring, potentially reducing semantic richness.
    • Intervention experiments demonstrate correlation between concept activation and output change, not causal attribution.
    • Only the first 15% of the LLaVA-NeXT dataset and the first 5000 SAE neurons are analyzed due to computational cost (~30 GPU-hours on 2× H100).
    • CLIP and ALIGN as evaluators introduce their own biases; black-masked patches degrade their reliability (requiring a blurring workaround).

    Relevance to Vision-Language Models¶

    This work advances mechanistic interpretability of VLMs by revealing that SAE latent neurons encode not just unimodal but genuinely cross-modal concepts—directly probing how models like LLaVA-NeXT integrate vision and language internally. The finding that multimodal concept interventions produce stronger output shifts than visual or textual ones alone has implications for understanding (and potentially steering) VLM reasoning. For researchers tracking VLMs, this provides a concrete diagnostic tool to audit which neurons are vision-dominated, language-dominated, or truly multimodal—relevant for debugging modality bias, improving alignment, and interpreting failure modes in VQA. The cross-modal grounding technique (adding text to visual queries) also suggests a practical improvement to any SAE-based VLM analysis pipeline.
