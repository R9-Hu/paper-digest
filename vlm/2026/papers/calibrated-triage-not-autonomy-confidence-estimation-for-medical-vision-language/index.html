    Calibrated Triage, Not Autonomy: Confidence Estimation for Medical Vision-Language Models¶

    🕒 Published (v1): 2026-06-14 16:39 UTC · Source: Arxiv · link

    TL;DR¶

    Medical LVLMs can produce fluent, confident answers while barely attending to the image, making reliable confidence estimation essential for safe clinical deployment. This paper benchmarks seven confidence estimators across five open-weight LVLMs and three medical VQA datasets under out-of-distribution transfer (probes trained only on natural images), recast as bounded selective prediction. The central finding: standard metrics are poor deployment guides, the choice of estimator matters more than the choice of model, and the appropriate near-term role is calibrated triage—not autonomy.

    Problem¶

    Existing confidence estimators are tuned for general-domain ranking under uniform error cost, but clinical deployment requires a different objective: drawing a principled line between automatable cases (held below a tolerated error rate) and cases to defer to a clinician. No prior work systematically tests confidence estimators on medical VQA against this deployment objective under realistic out-of-distribution transfer (probe trained on natural images, tested on unseen medical scans), nor addresses grounding failure—where a model produces a confident answer without actually using the image.

    Method¶

    Datasets: GMAI-MMBench (broad clinical, n=21,748), SLAKE (radiology/anatomy, n=2,094), PATH-VQA (pathology, n=6,111). All trainable probes are trained on GQA (20k train, 5k val) natural-image VQA and evaluated on medical sets without any adaptation.

    Models: Five open-weight instruction-tuned LVLMs—Qwen3-VL-8B, LLaVA-NeXT-13B, InternVL3.5-14B, DeepSeek-VL2, Gemma-3-27B (4.5B–27B parameters), run with greedy decoding.

    Estimators (7 total): - Inference-only (black-box): P(True), Self-Probing, Prompt Ensemble - Trained internal probes: SAPLMA (post-answer hidden state), InternalInspector (attention+FF+residual encoder), CCPS (perturbation-based representation stability), BICR (grounding-aware contrastive probe that penalizes confidence carried over when the image is blacked out)

    Evaluation framework: Bounded selective prediction—automate only when confidence exceeds a threshold, defer the rest. Metrics: ECE (calibration), AUROC (discrimination), AURC (area under risk-coverage curve), safe yield Y@τ (largest automatable fraction holding error ≤τ), and top-decile error (ErrTop10%). Correctness labels assigned by a single gpt-5-mini judge for semantic equivalence.

    Key Contributions¶

    • First systematic out-of-distribution comparison of confidence estimators for medical LVLMs, across 7 estimators × 5 models × 3 datasets under one deployment-oriented protocol.
    • Reframes confidence estimation as bounded selective prediction, revealing a two-level structure: base-model competence sets a ceiling; the confidence layer determines how much of that ceiling is safely reachable.
    • Identifies that standard aggregate metrics (AUROC, average ECE) are poor guides—what matters is the high-confidence tail (top-decile error rate), which separates methods by 40+ percentage points.
    • Shows grounding-aware BICR is the only estimator that meaningfully lowers its confidence when the model answers without using the image, reducing confident-error rates to 1–4% vs. 41–45% for the weakest baselines.
    • Demonstrates that aggregate calibration can conceal fairness gaps (e.g., BICR performs well on common modalities but degrades on rarer ones: macro ECE 0.22 vs. micro ECE 0.11).

    Results¶

    • Base accuracy by domain: SLAKE 60.9%, GMAI-MMBench 47.2%, PATH-VQA 39.5%—ordered by distance from natural-image training distribution.
    • Calibration: Inference-only baselines ECE 0.31–0.47 (overconfident). BICR: ECE 0.11 on GMAI, 0.14 on PATH-VQA. InternalInspector: ECE 0.03 on SLAKE (best observed).
    • Discrimination: AUROC differences are small—best baseline P(True) at 61.9 vs. best probe at 62.9 on GMAI; Friedman test across 15 (LVLM × dataset) cells weakly significant (χ²=14.0, p=0.029), no per-method Wilcoxon vs. BICR survives Holm correction (p>0.2).
    • Top-decile error: P(True) confidently wrong on 29.2% of its top-confidence errors on GMAI, 10.0% on SLAKE vs. BICR at 25.2% on GMAI, 9.2% on SLAKE. CCPS worst overall (47–59%).
    • Safe yield at 20% error budget: SLAKE: BICR 34.6%, InternalInspector 23.8%, P(True) 29.5%; GMAI: BICR 4.5%, all others ≈0%; PATH-VQA: essentially 0% for all methods.
    • Oracle ceilings: SLAKE 76%, GMAI 59%, PATH-VQA 49% at Y@20—best real method captures 45% of SLAKE ceiling, ~8% of GMAI ceiling, ~0% of pathology ceiling.
    • Within-dataset replication: BICR safe yield correlates with per-department base accuracy in GMAI (r=0.85) and per-region accuracy in SLAKE (r=0.93); confidence AUROC also correlates with base accuracy (r=0.74 GMAI, r=0.88 SLAKE).
    • Temperature scaling: Reduces P(True) ECE from 0.32 to probe level on GMAI but leaves safe yield unchanged at ≈0%.
    • Pathology exception: On PATH-VQA, Prompt Ensemble achieves best AURC (0.490) and P(True) best top-decile error (33.8%)—grounding-aware probe (BICR: 0.532 AURC, 47.3% top-decile error) offers no tail-safety advantage, making expensive probes unjustified there.

    Limitations¶

    • Correctness labels use a single gpt-5-mini judge not separately validated on these medical sets; subtle semantic equivalence in clinical language is precisely where the judge may err.
    • All probes evaluated strictly out-of-distribution (trained on natural images); in-domain medical fine-tuning of the confidence layer is unstudied.
    • Safe-yield thresholds (10–20% error budgets) are first approximations; deploying sites must re-validate against their own case mix and institutional harm profiles.
    • All datasets are VQA over still images; dynamic or report-generation settings are untested.
    • Absolute discrimination is modest across all methods (AUROC ≈0.63 for best), so the usable advantage is calibration and bounded-error triage rather than strong ranking.
    • No single estimator dominates across domains or models, requiring per-site, per-domain validation—limiting generalizability of pooled recommendations.

    Relevance to Vision-Language Models¶

    This paper directly addresses a structural weakness of medical LVLMs: the ability to produce fluent, high-confidence outputs driven by language priors while bypassing visual input entirely. For VLM researchers, it provides concrete evidence that grounding fidelity—whether the model actually used the image—must be a first-class design criterion for safety-critical deployments, not an emergent property of architecture. The finding that standard AUROC and average ECE mislead in triage settings challenges common VLM evaluation practice and argues for domain-specific, threshold-oriented metrics (AURC, safe yield, top-decile error) as deployment proxies. The two-level competence-calibration framework is broadly applicable: as general-purpose VLMs are fine-tuned for specialized domains, this work suggests the confidence layer—particularly grounding-aware calibration—will be the binding constraint on safe automation before model accuracy itself.
