# Cross-modal Associations in Vision and Language Models: Revisiting the Bouba-Kiki Effect

## TL;DR
This paper probes whether CLIP (ResNet-50 and ViT variants) exhibits the bouba-kiki effect—the human tendency to associate pseudowords like "bouba" with round shapes and "kiki" with jagged ones. Using two complementary methods (probability-based prompting and Grad-CAM visual attention), neither CLIP variant shows consistent, human-like cross-modal associations between pseudoword phonology and visual shape features.

## Problem
Prior work on VLMs and the bouba-kiki effect is conflicting: Alper & Averbuch-Elor (2023) reported strong evidence in CLIP/Stable Diffusion, while subsequent studies found no robust effect, especially cross-lingually. The field lacked a rigorous, human-experiment-aligned evaluation that (a) uses carefully controlled image pairs, (b) employs interpretability tools to check whether correct predictions are made for the right reasons, and (c) enables direct quantitative comparison to human behavioral data.

## Method
Two CLIP variants (ResNet-50 and ViT) are probed with four linguistic label sources: original bouba-kiki/maluma-takete pseudowords, English adjectives (sharp/curved synonyms as baseline), Nielsen & Rendall-style constructed pseudowords (sonorant-rounded vs. plosive-non-rounded), and Alper & Averbuch-Elor-style generated pseudowords. Visual inputs are curved/jagged shape pairs sourced from prior human studies and newly generated by connecting random points with curved vs. straight line segments.

**Method 1 (Probability probing):** For each image, the label with the highest CLIP probability across the pseudoword set is taken as the model's preference; a "match" requires both images in a pair to be correctly paired with their congruent labels. Ten prompt templates are tested for robustness. Bayesian regression models assess above-chance performance.

**Method 2 (Grad-CAM attention):** Image pairs are concatenated into a single image. Grad-CAM gradients are computed against the cosine similarity between label and image embeddings, extracting spatial attention maps from the final convolutional layer (ResNet) or CLS-to-patch attention (ViT). The model's "choice" is the image region (left/right) receiving higher summed attention, directly mirroring Ćwiek et al. (2022)'s forced-choice human experiment.

## Key Contributions
- Comprehensive re-evaluation of the bouba-kiki effect in CLIP using methods maximally aligned with human psycholinguistic experiments, enabling direct human–model comparisons.
- Novel application of Grad-CAM to cross-modal association testing, going beyond behavioral performance to probe whether models attend to the shape features causally relevant to the effect.
- Demonstrates that prior positive results (Alper & Averbuch-Elor) likely reflect language-specific semantic regularities between word forms and meaning, not genuine sensorimotor cross-modal mappings.
- Shows that CLIP's individual modalities can separately distinguish round vs. jagged (shapes encodable, adjectives matchable), isolating the failure to the cross-modal integration step.

## Results
- **English adjective baseline:** ~80% correct shape-word matching across both models and prompts—confirms models can process shape semantics but at a surprisingly imperfect level.
- **Pseudoword probability probing:** Performance collapses to near-chance for all pseudoword types (bouba-kiki, maluma-takete, constructed, generated) across both model variants; descriptive means are mostly at the 50% chance line.
- **Grad-CAM vs. humans:** Human bouba-kiki accuracy (Ćwiek et al., 2022) far exceeds both models; neither CLIP variant reliably exceeds chance (25% for paired matching) on the classic bouba-kiki or maluma-takete label pairs.
- **ResNet Grad-CAM:** Shows a general round-shape bias across all word types including jagged-associated pseudowords—no differential cross-modal mapping.
- **ViT Grad-CAM:** Reliably distinguishes shapes for English adjectives but collapses to chance for pseudowords, showing the weakest cross-modal alignment of the two variants.
- **Attention qualitatively:** Grad-CAM heatmaps predominantly focus on shape centers or low-information background regions rather than edge/curvature features relevant to bouba-kiki discrimination.
- Models select from a narrow label distribution (~35% unique labels chosen), indicating predictions are largely independent of image content.

## Limitations
- Evaluation restricted to CLIP (ResNet-50, ViT); proprietary models (GPT-4o, Gemini) and post-CLIP alignment layers in full VLMs (LLaVA, BLIP2, InternVL) are untested.
- Uses text-based pseudowords rather than audio; the original bouba-kiki effect is acoustic, though orthographic and auditory contributions are tightly coupled in literate participants.
- Grad-CAM applied to ViT requires adaptation (CLS-to-patch attention from final attention block) that may not perfectly capture feature attribution as it does in convolutional architectures.
- Image set is limited (17 pairs for Grad-CAM analysis); generalization across a wider shape space is not established.
- Does not test whether supervised fine-tuning or cross-modal alignment layers built on top of CLIP can recover the effect.

## Relevance to Vision-Language Models
This paper directly interrogates whether CLIP—the frozen vision backbone underlying BLIP2, LLaVA, InternVL, and Molmo—encodes the kind of grounded, cross-modal feature integration that characterizes human multimodal cognition. The negative result implies that the inability to perform non-arbitrary form-meaning mapping is an architectural and training-objective limitation inherited by a large class of downstream VLMs, not just a surface-level benchmark gap. The Grad-CAM finding that models fail to attend to the very visual features (edges, curvature) necessary for the task points to a deeper failure in visuo-linguistic grounding, relevant to known VLM weaknesses in spatial reasoning and compositional understanding. For researchers tracking VLMs, this work provides a psycholinguistically rigorous probe methodology that can complement standard benchmarks when diagnosing cross-modal representation quality.
