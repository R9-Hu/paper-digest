    🆕 Today's Digest — Agentic AI / LLM Agents¶

    New papers/posts collected 2026-06-25.

    20 new today

    📋 In brief¶

    This batch is dominated by a deep dive into reinforcement learning for agentic systems, with nearly half the papers diagnosing or fixing how RL post-training goes wrong: credit assignment failures (SCPO, BiPACE, OPID), training collapse from control-token probability spikes, task insensitivity from attention drift, and the free signal hiding in the log-probability ratio between policy and reference. A second strong thread is safety and reliability — from formalizing natural-language policies into deterministic Cedar rules and dissecting MCP poisoning attacks, to adaptive red-teaming of prompt-injection defenses and a proposed forensics discipline for distinguishing misalignment from mere confusion. Practical limits of current approaches surface repeatedly: tool-calling silently breaks under constrained decoding, all 12 frontier models score below 60% on reliability-hazard benchmarks, and the co-failure ceiling paper proves mathematically that routing or voting over any pool of models is bounded by how often they all fail together — a ceiling that correlations systematically underestimate. Tying it together is a recurring honesty about gaps: Heuresis finds that search strategies can trade off quality, diversity, and novelty but cannot push the frontier outward, and AAWM argues that next-observation prediction has been the wrong world-modeling objective all along.

    📝 Today's papers¶

    Autoformalization of Agent Instructions into Policy-as-Code ICML 2026¶

    LLM-powered agents need formal safety guarantees that probabilistic guardrails (classifiers, prompt steering) cannot provide. This paper introduces an autoformalization pipeline that converts natural-language agent instructions, MCP tool schemas, and policy documents into Cedar Policy Language rules via a generator-critic loop, enforcing them at runtime through an external deterministic policy engine. On MedAgentBench, the pipeline covers substantially more of an 88-rule policy than hand-coded symbolic guardrails covering only 23 of those rules.

    📌 Why selected: ICML 2026; first formal Policy-as-Code autoformalization for agent safety with hard guarantees

    When Does Combining Language Models Help? A Co-Failure Ceiling on Routing, Voting, and Mixture-of-Agents Across 67 Frontier Models¶

    Any selection policy over a pool of LLMs (routing, voting, cascades, mixture-of-agents) has accuracy bounded by \(1 - \beta\), where \(\beta\) is the rate at which all models fail on the same query simultaneously. The industry diagnostic—mean pairwise error correlation \(\rho\)—cannot identify \(\beta\) and systematically underprices the co-failure tail, especially as pool size grows. Across 67 frontier models, a correctly calibrated Gaussian copula still underestimates the true co-failure tail by ~2.5×, and learned routers capture near-zero of the oracle gain.

    📌 Why selected: Co-failure ceiling theorem fundamentally bounds routing/voting/MoA gain across 67 frontier models

    Localizing RL-Induced Tool Use to a Single Crosscoder Feature ICML 2026¶

    RL fine-tuning for tool use concentrates its mechanistic signal into a surprisingly compact feature set that can be localized via Dedicated Feature Crosscoders (DFC). Steering a single DFC-exclusive feature achieves +65 pp tool-correctness on Qwen2.5-3B with zero retraining. Jointly training the crosscoder also leaks tool-calling capability into the frozen base model (+6.8 pp), revealing that RL-induced representations partially inhabit shared decoder directions.

    📌 Why selected: ICML 2026; mechanistic localization of RL-induced tool use to single crosscoder feature

    ShareLock: A Stealthy Multi-Tool Threshold Poisoning Attack Against MCP¶

    ShareLock is a multi-tool poisoning attack against MCP-based LLM agents that fragments adversarial prompts into cryptographic shares using Shamir's Secret Sharing and distributes them across multiple tool descriptions, achieving information-theoretic stealth. A covert reconstruction trigger, introduced via a server update, causes the agent to reassemble and execute the hidden instruction. Experiments across four domains and two MCP clients show >90% average attack success rate while evading mainstream safety classifiers.

    📌 Why selected: Threshold poisoning attack on MCP multi-tool chains; directly threatens harness security

    Empowering GUI Agents via Autonomous Experience Exploration and Hindsight Experience Utilization for Task Planning ACL 2026¶

    PEEU (Planning Experience Exploration and Utilization) trains small open-source MLLMs (3B/7B) for GUI web navigation by having an agent autonomously explore websites, then using hindsight to retroactively align tasks to the actual collected trajectories rather than forcing trajectories to match pre-specified goals. A 7B model trained with 2k trajectories reaches 30.6% on WebVoyager, surpassing Qwen2.5-VL-32B (22.7%).

    📌 Why selected: ACL 2026; GUI agents with autonomous hindsight experience for planning in open-source MLLMs

    OPID: On-Policy Skill Distillation for Agentic Reinforcement Learning¶

    OPID augments outcome-based RL for LLM agents by extracting hierarchical "skills" (episode-level workflows and step-level critical-decision rules) directly from completed on-policy trajectories, then distilling them as dense token-level advantages without requiring external skill libraries. The key insight is that hindsight skills derived from the policy's own rollouts are distribution-matched by construction, sidestepping the state-drift problem of retrieved off-policy skills. OPID consistently outperforms both outcome-only RL (GRPO) and skill-distillation baselines across three multi-step agentic benchmarks.

    📌 Why selected: On-policy skill distillation adds dense signal to sparse outcome-based RL for language agents

    Joint Learning of Experiential Rules and Policies for Large Language Model Agents¶

    JERP jointly updates a natural-language experiential-rule pool and the LLM policy parameters from the same interaction trajectories, so the two stay mutually aligned throughout training. After each episode, sampled trajectories drive a GRPO-style parameter update and a contrastive-reflection rule-pool edit, letting stable behaviors gradually migrate into model weights while the rule pool adapts to the evolving policy. Experiments on AlfWorld and WebShop show consistent gains over both pure prompt-based and pure RL fine-tuning baselines.

    📌 Why selected: Unified framework jointly learning in-context rules and parametric policies from agent experience

    Diagnosing Task Insensitivity in Language Agents¶

    LLM agents trained on interactive tasks develop task insensitivity: they reconstruct and execute familiar task patterns even when the task description is corrupted or replaced with a semantically distinct task. The authors diagnose this via attention drift during training and propose Task-Perturbed NLL Optimization, a lightweight contrastive regularizer that explicitly penalizes high action probability under a replaced task description.

    📌 Why selected: Diagnoses task insensitivity as key OOD failure mode in long-horizon LLM agents

    Adaptive Evaluation of Out-of-Band Defenses Against Prompt Injection in LLM Agents¶

    Out-of-band (action-level) defenses against prompt injection in LLM agents — CaMeL, FIDES, Progent, RTBAS, FORGE — are validated only on static attack benchmarks, the same methodology that caused twelve in-band defenses to collapse under adaptive attacks. This paper organizes these defenses through classical security primitives (Biba integrity, reference monitors, least privilege) and runs an independent adaptive evaluation of Progent on an open-weight agent (Qwen2.5-7B), finding the deterministic gate held across three runs despite a hand-crafted defense-aware attack.

    📌 Why selected: Adaptive red-teaming evaluation framework for out-of-band prompt-injection defenses in tool agents

    The Interplay of Harness Design and Post-Training in LLM Agents¶

    This paper systematically studies how the design of the agent harness—the scaffolding that controls what tools, descriptions, and auxiliary state are shown to an LLM—interacts with RL-based post-training. Using an extended ALFWorld benchmark with controllable harness levels and tool schema shifts, the authors show that informative harnesses are necessary during (not just after) post-training to achieve both in-distribution performance and OOD robustness.

    📌 Why selected: Directly studies harness scaffolding + tool-description effect on agent performance; core to harness builders

    Model Forensics: Investigating Whether Concerning Behavior Reflects Misalignment¶

    Model forensics is a proposed discipline for investigating why a caught AI model took a concerning action — specifically, whether it reflects malign intent or a benign cause like confusion. The authors present a two-step baseline protocol (CoT reading → environment interventions) and apply it to six agentic environments, producing concrete case studies on Kimi K2 Thinking and DeepSeek R1.

    📌 Why selected: Neel Nanda (DeepMind); disentangles misalignment from behavior—critical for safe agent deployment

    Why Multi-Step Tool-Use Reinforcement Learning Collapses and How Supervisory Signals Fix It¶

    Reinforcement learning for multi-step tool-use in LLMs suffers a structural collapse failure mode—not capability loss—driven by probability spikes in special control tokens (e.g., <tool_call>, <|im_end|>). The paper systematically evaluates six supervisory signal strategies across synchronous and interleaved training paradigms, finding that interleaved SFT+RL (especially Process Reflection Supervision) substantially stabilizes training. A new Process Reflection Supervision method that converts intermediate trajectory errors into textual feedback achieves the best results.

    📌 Why selected: Identifies root cause of multi-step tool-use RL collapse; supervisory-signal fix immediately applicable

    Autodata: An agentic data scientist to create high quality synthetic data¶

    Autodata frames synthetic data creation as an agentic loop: an LLM "data scientist" iteratively generates data, analyzes quality via weak/strong solver discrimination, synthesizes feedback into an improved generation recipe, and can itself be meta-optimized. The core instantiation, Agentic Self-Instruct, consistently outperforms static CoT Self-Instruct baselines across CS research QA, legal reasoning, and mathematical reasoning tasks.

    📌 Why selected: Meta AI; agents meta-trained as data scientists; generalizable recipe for synthetic data generation

    Neglected Free Lunch from Post-training: Progress Advantage for LLM Agents¶

    RL post-training already encodes a free, annotation-free process-level scoring signal: the log-probability ratio between the RL-trained policy and its reference policy exactly recovers the optimal advantage function under a stochastic MDP. The authors term this progress advantage and show it outperforms dedicated trained process reward models (PRMs) on test-time scaling, uncertainty quantification, and failure attribution across five benchmarks and four model families—with zero additional training.

    📌 Why selected: Practical process-reward approximation for long-horizon agents without expensive step-level annotation

    Semantic Consistency Policy Optimization for Reinforcement Learning of LLM Agents EMNLP 2026¶

    Group-based RL methods for LLM agents assign every step in a failed trajectory the same negative credit, even when some steps are nearly identical to those in successful rollouts—a failure mode the authors call semantic credit inconsistency. SCPO is a reward-shaping plugin that recovers step-level credit for failed steps by matching them against a successful sibling in the same rollout group using a frozen cross-encoder. At 1.5B parameters, SCPO reaches 93.7% on ALFWorld and 74.8% task-success on WebShop, outperforming GiGPO by +7.0 and +9.8 points respectively.

    📌 Why selected: EMNLP 2026; semantic consistency resolves step-level credit assignment in sparse-reward agentic RL

    BiPACE: Bisimulation-Guided Policy Optimization with Action Counterfactual Estimation for LLM Agents¶

    BiPACE is a drop-in advantage estimator for stepwise group-based RL that fixes a state-action credit mismatch in methods like GiGPO: exact observation hashing creates too many singleton clusters (zero gradient), and a single within-group mean ignores action-specific credit. It replaces observation hashing with bisimulation-guided hidden-state clustering (BiGPO) and adds action-conditioned peer baselines (PACE), achieving 97.1±0.9% on ALFWorld/Qwen2.5-7B vs. GiGPO's 90.8%, with only 11.3% per-step overhead.

    📌 Why selected: Bisimulation + counterfactual estimation fixes systematic bias in group-relative LLM agent RL

    Beyond Function Calling: Benchmarking Tool-Using Agents under Tool-Environment Unreliability¶

    ToolBench-X is a benchmark for evaluating LLM-based tool-using agents under five structured "reliability hazard" types injected into otherwise clean tool environments. Every hazard instance remains solvable via at least one recovery path, isolating agent robustness from task infeasibility. All 12 evaluated frontier models score below 60%, with diagnosis of the hazard—not compute budget—being the primary failure bottleneck.

    📌 Why selected: Benchmark for tool agents under real-world unreliability; honest evaluation beyond clean-environment assumptions

    Beyond Next-Observation Prediction: Agent-Authored World Modeling for Sequential Decision Making¶

    AAWM (Agent-Authored World Modeling) replaces next-observation prediction as the world modeling objective for LLM agents with agent-authored targets that capture what the policy actually needs to know before acting. At each state, the policy self-probes its beliefs and open questions, retrieves cross-trajectory evidence, and synthesizes decision-oriented training targets. AAWM consistently outperforms next-observation prediction across environments, model scales, and training regimes.

    📌 Why selected: Agent-authored world modeling objective; richer supervision signal than next-observation prediction

    Constraint Tax in Open-Weight LLMs: An Empirical Study of Tool Calling Suppression Under Structured Output Constraints¶

    When Tool Calling and JSON Schema–constrained Structured Output are enabled simultaneously in open-weight LLM inference, models silently stop invoking tools while still producing schema-compliant responses — a failure mode the authors term Tool Suppression. The root cause is grammar-based token masking in constrained decoding frameworks (vLLM, SGLang), which renders tool-call tokens unreachable at the decoding layer. A training-free Transparent Two-Pass Execution strategy restores tool invocation by decoupling the two phases at inference time.

    📌 Why selected: Production-reproduced: structured output constraints suppress tool calling in open-weight LLMs

    Heuresis: Search Strategies for Autonomous AI Research Agents Across Quality, Diversity and Novelty¶

    Heuresis is an open-source framework that abstracts autonomous ML research into composable primitives and implements six pluggable search strategies (greedy, MAP-Elites, Go-Explore, Islands, Curiosity, Omni) evaluated across quality, diversity, and novelty. Across 3,222 scored runs on three domains, no generated idea is rated "Original" and novel ideas never reach the quality of known-recipe solutions. The study reveals a fundamental gap: current search and Quality-Diversity methods can steer the quality–diversity–novelty trade-off but cannot expand the quality–novelty frontier.

    📌 Why selected: Search strategies balancing quality/diversity/novelty for autonomous AI research agents
