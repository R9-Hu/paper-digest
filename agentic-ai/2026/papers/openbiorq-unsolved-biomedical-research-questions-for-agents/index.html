    OpenBioRQ: Unsolved Biomedical Research Questions for Agents¶

    🕒 Published (v1): 2026-06-20 00:00 UTC · Source: HuggingFace · link

    Why this paper was selected

    15.9% citation error rate in agentic models; critical evaluation finding for agent reliability

    TL;DR¶

    OpenBioRQ is a benchmark of 12,553 genuinely unsolved biomedical research questions designed to evaluate agentic LLMs on faithfulness and abstention rather than answer correctness. It occupies a previously empty quadrant—agentic × unsolved—where failure modes like wrong-paper citation and agentic collapse become visible only when no answer key exists. Frontier agents span a wide 29–60% solve range on the hardest subset, with none solving more than ~67%, confirming the benchmark is hard and non-saturating.

    Problem¶

    All existing biomedical QA benchmarks—closed-form (MedQA, BioASQ), long-form (OLAPH, HealthBench), or agentic (GeneGPT, BioMysteryBench)—assume a fixed known answer. This shared assumption hides three failure modes: (1) wrong-paper citation (a real PMID that does not support the claim), (2) agentic collapse (agents abandon tool use on hard questions), and (3) confabulation vs. abstention tradeoffs. When an answer key exists, models can echo pre-specified sources without independently verifying citation–claim alignment.

    Method¶

    Corpus construction. Questions are extracted from PubMed, trial registries, arXiv, JLA Priority Setting Partnerships, NICE research recommendations, WHO/CHNRI/NASEM Delphi documents, and Cochrane research-gap literature via a crawl → extract → refine → dedup pipeline. A refinement LLM converts raw fragments (e.g., "pathophysiology of PSVD remains unclear") into self-contained questions (+33.8 pp over extract-only). Near-duplicates are removed with MiniLM-L6 cosine ≥ 0.90.

    Openness verification. A retrieval-grounded Stage-2 judge re-evaluates each question against real follow-up evidence (not source framing), changing status on 56.5% of a 200-question sample. Source-framing-only judgment collapsed to 0% answered/unknown (confirmation bias). The 657-question core passes a contamination positive-control at 100% recall, 0% false-positive; membership tests find no memorization signature.

    Empirical difficulty. Three open-weight roster models (GLM-5.1, Qwen3.6, DeepSeek-V4) answer every question with full tool access; a question is core when all three fail (checklist score < 0.5). This yields 49% core, 45% discriminating, 6% easy on the priority-setting track.

    Evaluation harness. Models issue up to 10 rounds of interleaved reasoning and tool calls across 10 biomedical REST APIs (PubMed, ClinicalTrials.gov, OpenFDA, OpenTargets, ChEMBL, UniProt, PubChem, KEGG, NCBI Datasets, BioMCP). Each answer is graded against a frozen per-question checklist \(C_q = \{(t_i, w_i)\}\) with criterion types {must mention, must acknowledge, must ground, must avoid} and weights \(w_i \in \{1,2,3\}\):

    \[\text{score}(q) = \frac{\sum_{i \in I_q} w_i v_i}{\sum_{i \in I_q} w_i} \in [0,1]\]

    A model solves \(q\) iff score \(\geq 0.5\) at \(T=0\).

    Two-level citation audit. L1 checks identifier existence; L2 checks whether the resolved record actually supports the attached claim, using an LLM cross-family judge with Cohen's \(\kappa = 0.755\) inter-judge agreement on binary verdicts.

    Key Contributions¶

    • First benchmark in the agentic × unsolved quadrant: 12,553 genuinely open biomedical questions across 12 domains, with no answer key.
    • Retrieval-grounded openness verification that eliminates confirmation bias from source-framing-only labeling.
    • Empirical difficulty anchoring via three open-weight roster models rather than subjective labels.
    • Two-level citation audit separating L1 existence from L2 content support, exposing wrong-paper citation as the dominant failure mode in biomedicine (15.9% of resolved citations) vs. fabrication (0.7%).
    • Discovery of agentic collapse: on the hardest questions, agents stop issuing tool calls; for the most collapse-prone model, blocking tool access entirely barely changes performance.
    • Frozen per-question checklist scoring that raises inter-judge Spearman correlation from 0.35 to 0.82.

    Results¶

    • Citation L1 (existence): 99.3% of 4,863 citations resolve; only 0.7% fabricated (cf. ~54% in ResearchMath for math writing).
    • Citation L2 (content support): 15.9% of the 4,649 real citations are wrong-paper under the primary judge; 10.6% under an independent different-family judge (Opus-4.7); micro-average across models, macro-average ~30.0%.
    • Per-model L2: Qwen3.6: 20.2% wrong-paper; DeepSeek-V4: 13.1%; GLM-5.1: worst (but small sample, \(n=97\)).
    • Gold-answer misattribution: ~74% of LLM-synthesized gold answers' cited PMIDs do not support their claims (\(n \approx 360\), two-judge robust).
    • Frontier agent solve rates on 657-question full core (Wilson 95% CI): GPT-5.5: 66.7%; Opus-4.7: 48.6%; Gemini-3-Pro: 37.4%.
    • Frontier agent solve rates on 423-question frozen core: GPT-5.5: 59.6%; Opus-4.7: 37.8%; Gemini-3-Pro: 28.8%.
    • Roster models on priority-setting track: all three below 0.5 mean checklist score (GLM-5.1: 0.31, Qwen3.6: 0.32, DeepSeek-V4: 0.45).
    • Held-out models from same lineage as difficulty anchors: solve only ~17% of the core.
    • Checklist vs. free-form scoring: inter-judge Spearman \(\rho\) rises from 0.35 to 0.82 with frozen checklist.
    • Self-containment: 85.4% overall (retrieval-verified: 95%; expert-consensus: 75%); extract-only baseline: 51.6%.

    Limitations¶

    • L2 wrong-paper verdict uses an LLM cross-family judge proxy; agreement with human medical experts is deferred to future work (human annotation set released for this purpose).
    • The retrieval-grounded openness detector has unquantified recall for narrative or guideline resolution—it confirmed 0/657 core questions resolved, but cannot rule out narrative evidence it cannot retrieve.
    • Inter-judge agreement (Spearman 0.82) is LLM-vs-LLM; human-expert checklist agreement is not yet measured.
    • The benchmark targets research assistance (evidence retrieval, faithful citation), not clinical decision support; results must not be used to guide patient care.
    • Agentic collapse severity varies across models and is characterized descriptively rather than causally.
    • The 15.9% wrong-paper rate relies on micro-averaging (dominated by DeepSeek-V4 volume); macro-average (~30%) is inflated by GLM-5.1's small sample.
    • A claim-extraction bug affecting ~11% of L2 snippets required correction; the fix is documented but introduces a pipeline artifact.

    Relevance to Agentic AI / LLM Agents¶

    OpenBioRQ directly probes two underexplored failure modes of tool-using agents: citation faithfulness (real identifiers pointing to wrong papers) and agentic collapse (agents abandoning tool use on exactly the queries where tools matter most). The benchmark's unsolved-question design is a methodological advance for the field because it removes the answer-key shortcut that lets agents pass evaluations by echoing pre-specified sources rather than independently verifying claim–evidence alignment. The observed 29–60% solve-rate spread across frontier agents on the same hard subset confirms that current benchmarks with known answers are already near-saturated as discriminators, while this regime remains challenging and differentiating. For researchers building or evaluating multi-step reasoning agents, the frozen-checklist scoring approach—decomposing open-ended grading into auditable must-mention/must-acknowledge/must-ground/must-avoid verdicts—offers a transferable template for evaluating agents on tasks without ground-truth answers.
