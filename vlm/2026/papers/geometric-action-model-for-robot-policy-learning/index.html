    Geometric Action Model for Robot Policy Learning¶

    🕒 Published (v1): 2026-06-15 17:58 UTC · Source: Arxiv · link

    TL;DR¶

    GAM repurposes a pretrained Geometric Foundation Model (GFM) as a shared backbone for robot manipulation by splitting it at an intermediate layer and inserting a causal transformer that jointly predicts future 3D latent geometry and action chunks. This design embeds explicit 3D spatial priors throughout the entire predictive pipeline rather than treating geometry as a static external feature extractor. Compared to VLA and video world-action model baselines, GAM achieves higher robustness to camera perturbations, faster inference (6.9 ms / ~145 Hz), and smaller model size (1.4B parameters).

    Problem¶

    Existing Vision-Language-Action models (VLAs) and Video World-Action Models (WAMs) operate primarily in 2D image space or 2D-derived latent spaces, leaving 3D cues (depth, scale, occlusion) implicit. Prior geometry-aware VLAs that incorporate GFMs use them only as static feature extractors via representation distillation or encoder replacement, never exploiting the GFM's layered internal structure for temporal prediction or action decoding. This limits out-of-distribution robustness, particularly under camera viewpoint perturbations.

    Method¶

    GAM splits a pretrained GFM (DA3-Giant) at an intermediate layer (Ls = 12, where frame-wise attention transitions to cross-view global attention):

    1. Observation Encoder: Shallow GFM layers (blocks 0–Ls) extract geometric latent tokens from multi-view RGB inputs across a context window of H timesteps.
    2. Causal Future Predictor: A 12-layer causal transformer inserted at the split layer takes the stacked geometric tokens augmented with proprioceptive state tokens, previous action tokens, and frozen T5 language tokens. It autoregressively predicts (a) future latent geometric tokens Z̃(Ls)_{t+1} and (b) a next-action token ã_t, using block-causal self-attention to prevent future leakage.
    3. Feature Propagation & Decoding: Predicted future tokens and per-view action tokens are concatenated and passed through the remaining deep GFM blocks (Ls+1–M). A lightweight action regression head aggregates action tokens into the executed action chunk (C=8 steps, da=7 DoF), while the original DPT depth head decodes future depth maps.

    Training uses a three-term loss: L1 action regression (λ=3), L1 alignment of predicted vs. frozen-GFM future latent tokens (λ=1), and scale-invariant + gradient-matching depth loss (λ=3). Only GFM blocks 13–39, the causal predictor, and the action head are trained (~983M of 1404M parameters). Pretraining uses 784K trajectories from Open-X-Embodiment (72%), MimicGen (18%), and RoboCasa365 (10%); real-world depth supervision uses pseudo-labels from the frozen GFM backbone.

    Key Contributions¶

    • Single shared-backbone architecture that unifies 3D perception, causal future geometry prediction, and action decoding inside a GFM without a separate large language model or video diffusion model.
    • Joint autoregressive sequence for both action tokens and future-scene geometry tokens, decoded in one forward pass rather than via separate heads or iterative diffusion.
    • Demonstrated that inserting the causal predictor at the GFM's frame-to-global attention boundary (Ls=12) is critical; layers too shallow or too deep cause complete performance collapse.
    • Strong out-of-distribution generalization: geometry priors enable robustness to camera viewpoint, lighting, background, and scene layout perturbations.

    Results¶

    • LIBERO-Plus (camera perturbation): GAM 83.1% vs. next-best π0.5 76.6% (+6.5%p); +9.7%p improvement cited in abstract over the best geometry-aware VLA baseline (Spatial Forcing 0.1% on that split).
    • LIBERO-Plus overall: GAM 85.5% (↓12.1 from in-distribution); π0.5 84.6% (↓12.3), Cosmos-Policy 82.4% (↓16.1), OpenVLA-OFT 69.6% (↓27.5).
    • LIBERO in-distribution: GAM 97.6%, matching saturated VLA baselines (π0.5 96.9%, OpenVLA-OFT 97.1%, Cosmos-Policy 98.5%).
    • Inference latency (single GH200, matched Torch Compile, no CUDA Graphs): GAM 17.5 ms vs. π0.5 29.2 ms, OpenVLA-OFT 70.1 ms, Cosmos-Policy 382.4 ms; with CUDA Graphs: GAM 6.9 ms (55× faster than Cosmos-Policy).
    • Model size: 1.4B vs. π0.5 3.3B, OpenVLA-OFT 7B, Cosmos-Policy 2B.
    • Real-world (4 tasks, 20 trials each, ID + OOD camera): GAM substantially outperforms π0.5 and Spatial Forcing across all tasks and perturbation conditions (exact per-task numbers in Figure 4).
    • RoboCasa-Kitchen: 69.4% average across 24 tasks (zero-shot generalization from pretraining).

    Limitations¶

    • Language reasoning is bounded by the frozen T5 encoder; open-vocabulary instruction following or commonsense reasoning cannot benefit from a large language model's capabilities.
    • Pretraining required 64 GH200 GPUs for ~96 hours; fine-tuning each benchmark requires 16 GPUs for ~48 hours, making iteration expensive.
    • Real-world depth supervision relies on pseudo-labels from the frozen GFM backbone rather than ground-truth geometry, introducing label noise.
    • Evaluation is limited to single-arm tabletop manipulation; generalization to bimanual or mobile settings is untested.
    • Split layer selection is architecture-sensitive: performance collapses entirely at Ls ∈ {27, 33, 39}, requiring careful tuning per backbone.

    Relevance to Vision-Language Models¶

    GAM is directly relevant to the VLA subfield as a competitor and architectural contrast: instead of building on a VLM backbone (as in OpenVLA, π0.5, π0-FAST), it replaces the VLM substrate with a Geometric Foundation Model, trading open-vocabulary linguistic depth for explicit 3D spatial priors. This surfaces a core design choice for VLA researchers—whether semantic or spatial priors are the binding constraint for contact-rich manipulation generalization. The result that geometry-aligned feature distillation into existing VLAs (Spatial Forcing, ROCKET) underperforms end-to-end GFM repurposing (0.1% vs. 83.1% camera perturbation) challenges the current dominant paradigm of bolting geometric modules onto frozen VLM backbones. For those tracking VLM efficiency, GAM also establishes a pareto frontier: a 1.4B single-pass model matching or exceeding 7B+ VLA baselines on robustness benchmarks at 55× lower latency.
