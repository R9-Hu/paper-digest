    📋 In brief¶

    This batch is dominated by the construction and validation of multi-agent orchestration harnesses — systems where a meta-controller or orchestrator delegates subtasks to pools of specialized LLM agents, then synthesizes their outputs into a coherent result. A recurring structural pattern is the closed feedback loop: adversarial co-refinement (test-patch vs. code-patch generators), iterative self-verification cycles, and designer–coder–reflector triads that treat workflow design itself as an optimization target. Domain coverage is remarkably broad — drug discovery, surgical robotics, IChO/IPhO Olympiads, histopathology, EHR coding, chaos engineering, and schema linking all receive bespoke harnesses — yet the architectural vocabulary is nearly universal: a planner, role-specialized executors, optional RAG grounding, and a verifier or judge layer. A secondary thread questions where multi-agent complexity actually helps: several papers find that richer orchestration raises consistency and safety scores dramatically (MedBench v4: 54 → 80) while others show that simpler single-pass prompting can outperform elaborate pipelines on narrower tasks, signaling that harness design tradeoffs remain an open empirical question.

    📝 Today's papers¶

    Watts and Debts of Agentic Frameworks: An Empirical Study (Registered Report)¶

    This registered report proposes a confirmatory empirical study correlating Self-Admitted Technical Debt (SATD) density in agentic AI framework source code with hardware-level runtime energy consumption. The study evaluates five open-source frameworks (AutoGen, LangGraph, Agno, Google ADK, SmolAgents) across 20 AgentBench tasks on three hardware configurations. If SATD density predicts energy overhead, source-code analysis could become a low-cost proxy for energy-aware framework selection before any execution.

    📌 Why selected: Empirical study linking technical debt and energy costs in agentic framework deployments

    INFRAMIND: Infrastructure-Aware Multi-Agent Orchestration¶

    INFRAMIND is a multi-agent LLM orchestration framework that addresses "infrastructure blindness" — the failure of existing orchestrators to consider runtime GPU cluster state (queue depths, KV-cache pressure, latencies) when selecting models and topologies. It introduces a three-component hierarchical constrained MDP solved via end-to-end RL: an infra-aware planner, a per-step executor that jointly picks model and reasoning depth, and an EDF scheduler for within-queue prioritization. Across five benchmarks and three load regimes, it dominates all baselines on both accuracy and SLO compliance.

    📌 Why selected: INFRAMIND infrastructure-aware orchestration; topology selection using runtime serving state

    Self-Harness: Harnesses That Improve Themselves¶

    Self-Harness introduces a paradigm in which an LLM-based agent iteratively improves its own operating harness—without human engineers or a stronger external model—by mining failure patterns from execution traces, proposing minimal targeted edits, and validating them via regression testing. Applied to Terminal-Bench-2.0 with three heterogeneous base models, it yields absolute held-out pass-rate gains of up to 21.4 percentage points. The result is a model-specific harness that generalizes to unseen tasks rather than overfitting to observed failures.

    📌 Why selected: Harnesses that self-improve; directly addresses meta-harness adaptation to diverse LLM behaviors

    What makes a harness a harness: necessary and sufficient conditions for an agent harness¶

    This paper proposes a constitutive definition of the agent harness — the runtime engineering layer that wraps a language model and turns it into a coding agent — by deriving necessary and sufficient conditions from first principles. It operationalizes the definition as a four-test inclusion/exclusion procedure and applies it to six real harnesses (Claude Code, Codex CLI, Aider, Cline, OpenHands, SWE-agent) to demonstrate consistent classification. The core contribution is terminological precision: a shared vocabulary and a reproducible membership test for a concept that is widely used but loosely defined.

    📌 Why selected: Foundational: necessary and sufficient conditions defining what constitutes an agent harness

    AlloSpatial: Agentic Harness Framework for Spatial Reasoning in Foundation Models¶

    AlloSpatial is an agentic framework that equips Multimodal Foundation Models (MFMs) with allocentric spatial cognition by coupling a cognitive mapping sandbox (World2Mind) with a structured three-stage Spatial Reasoning Harness. The harness enforces tool invocation judgment, modality-decoupled evidence collection, and geometry–semantics arbitration before committing to an answer. This protocol is internalized into open-weight Qwen3-VL via supervised cold-start followed by GSPO reinforcement learning with a harness-gated trajectory reward.

    📌 Why selected: Agentic harness framework for spatial reasoning in foundation models; novel allocentric approach

    SearchSwarm: Towards Delegation Intelligence in Agentic LLMs for Long-Horizon Deep Research¶

    SearchSwarm introduces a harness that elicits high-quality delegation behavior from a main LLM agent—decomposing long-horizon research tasks and dispatching subtasks to subagents running in fresh, independent contexts—and then uses the resulting trajectories as supervised fine-tuning data to internalize "delegation intelligence" into model weights. The resulting 30B-A3B model achieves 68.1 on BrowseComp and 82.5 on GAIA, state-of-the-art among comparable-scale models and competitive with frontier closed-source systems.

    📌 Why selected: ICLR SearchSwarm; delegation intelligence for long-horizon deep research beyond context limits

    HARBOR: A Harness Framework for Agentic Robot Reinforcement Learning¶

    HARBOR frames robot RL automation as a harness-engineering problem, deploying a tuple \(H_{RL} = (H_A, C, M, G, K)\) — agents, commands, mutable artifacts, verifiable gates, and reusable knowledge — to automate the full sim-to-real workflow from dependency setup through policy training. Given only a natural-language task specification and a simulator codebase, HARBOR orchestrates specialized subagents through gate-checked, artifact-centric stages with parallel decentralized tuning trials and cross-run experience accumulation. The system achieves 48/50 stage success on ablation benchmarks while being the cheapest harness configuration tested.

    📌 Why selected: HARBOR harness framework for robot RL; bridges sim-to-real engineering pipeline gaps

    PerspectiveGap: A Benchmark for Multi-Agent Orchestration Prompting¶

    PerspectiveGap is a 110-scenario benchmark evaluating whether LLMs can write correct sub-agent prompts in multi-agent orchestrations — specifically, whether a main (orchestrator) agent assigns each sub-agent exactly the information fragments it needs, no more and no less. Across 27 commercial models, the average combined strict-pass rate is only 14.9%, with GPT-5.5 leading at 62.0% and claude-opus-4-7 scoring a weak 19.1% despite strong coding performance. This establishes orchestration prompting as a distinct, under-evaluated capability.

    📌 Why selected: PerspectiveGap benchmark for multi-agent orchestration prompting; measures sub-agent context gaps

    SKILL.nb: Selective Formalization and Gated Execution for Durable Agent Workflows¶

    SKILL.nb governs reusable agent workflows through evidence-calibrated lifecycle policies, deciding which workflow steps should be executable code versus natural-language-guided and when those choices should be revised as environments drift. It stores workflows as versioned Jupyter notebooks with interleaved NL guidance, executable cells, validation gates, fallback paths, and multimodal execution evidence. On WebArena-Verified it achieves 53.7% single-round success and retains 91.7% of successes across re-executions—15.5 pp above the next-best baseline.

    📌 Why selected: ICLR; selective formalization and gated execution for durable, lifecycle-reliable agent workflows

    Act As a Real Researcher: A Suite of Benchmarks Evaluating Frontier LLMs and Agentic Harnesses in Research Lifecycle¶

    AARRI-Bench is the first installment of the AARR benchmark series, comprising 82 manually crafted tasks designed to expose the gap between AI agents and human research interns by targeting researcher-quality behaviors—not just task completion.     📌 Why selected: Benchmarks frontier LLMs and agentic harnesses across full research lifecycle

    SCALE: Scalable Cross-Attention Learning with Extrapolation for Agentic Workflow Scheduling¶

    SCALE is a deep reinforcement learning scheduler for agentic LLM workflow DAGs on heterogeneous clusters that generalizes to unseen cluster sizes without retraining. The key insight is that permutation-invariant architecture alone is insufficient for scale generalization—a companion regularization method (SRR) must stabilize latent feature statistics as cluster size changes. Trained at \(N=16\) servers, SCALE achieves competitive scheduling at \(N=32\) and \(N=48\) without fine-tuning.

    📌 Why selected: SCALE cross-attention scheduler for DAG agentic workflows on heterogeneous clusters

    Where Instruction Hierarchy Breaks: Diagnosing and Repairing Failures in Reasoning Language Models¶

    This paper diagnoses instruction hierarchy (IH) failures in reasoning LLMs by decomposing compliance into three sequential stages—instruction identification, conflict resolution, and response realization—and proposes two training-free self-monitoring mechanisms (parallel input monitor and sequential output monitor) that reduce non-compliance by 81–99% on static attacks.
