    OmniGameArena: A Unified UE5 Benchmark for VLM Game Agents with Improvement Dynamics¶

    🕒 Published (v1): 2026-06-08 00:00 UTC · Source: HuggingFace · link

    Why this paper was selected

    Game arena benchmark with improvement dynamics; novel multi-agent VLM evaluation paradigm

    TL;DR¶

    OmniGameArena is a benchmark of twelve custom Unreal Engine 5 games spanning Solo, PvP, and Cooperative regimes, designed to evaluate VLM game agents under matched conditions with contamination controls. It introduces the Improvement Dynamics Curve (IDC), an agentic-reflection harness that tracks agent skill across multiple rounds rather than reporting a single cold-start score. Experiments across twelve agents reveal that no VLM dominates across games, commercial models lead open-weight models by a wide margin, and origin-task improvement does not reliably predict transfer to held-out variants.

    Problem¶

    Existing game benchmarks for VLM agents suffer from three gaps: (1) they report only a single first-attempt score per (agent, game) pair, hiding whether and how quickly agents improve under repeated interaction; (2) they focus almost exclusively on Solo play, leaving adversarial (PvP) and cooperative (Coop) regimes — which probe opponent modeling, role assignment, and teammate recovery — largely unmeasured; and (3) they reuse commercial game titles, creating pre-training contamination risk where agents may recall rules rather than reason about them.

    Method¶

    Benchmark construction. Twelve games are authored from scratch in UE5 — 7 Solo, 3 PvP, 2 Coop — so no game existed in public form before release. All share unified keyboard-mouse and gamepad interfaces, enabling heterogeneous agents (commercial VLMs, open-weight VLMs, specialized policies) to be evaluated under identical conditions. Contamination is empirically verified: a held-out VLM is shown screenshots and asked to name the game and describe its mechanics; OmniGameArena achieves 0.0% recognition and 50.0% mechanics leakage vs. 66–100% for prior benchmarks (Table 2). Per-episode progress is normalized to \([0, 1]\) via game-specific formulae (e.g., \(\frac{x_a - x_{\text{start}}}{x_{\text{finish}} - x_{\text{start}}}\) for navigation, \(\frac{D_e}{H_{\text{total}}}\) for combat).

    IDC harness. At round \(r\) a frozen VLM \(\pi_\theta\) acts as \(a_t \sim \pi_\theta(a_t \mid o_t, H_t, m_r)\), where \(m_r\) is a bounded skill prompt (≤1200 tokens) fixed throughout the round. The round score is \(S_r = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K} s(\tau_{r,k})\) over \(K\) episodes. A separate reflector LLM then runs four autonomous stages — Explore (reads trajectories via sandboxed read-only tools), Diagnose (commits failure modes via submit_diagnosis), Validate (proposes updated skill, verified by an independent LLM judge up to 5 times), Distill (writes accepted skill \(m_{r+1}\) and optionally updates a 2000-token experience notebook). The full sequence \([S_0, S_1, \ldots, S_R]\) forms the IDC. Best-skill rollback resets to \(m_{r^*}\) when \(S_{r+1} < \alpha \cdot S_{r^*}\) (\(\alpha = 0.5\)), guarding against catastrophic skill drift.

    Clock modes. To decouple inference latency from decision quality, two evaluation modes are defined: Paused Decision Quality (PDQ, environment frozen during inference) and Latency-Controlled Real-Time (LCRT, idles for server-reported inference time before applying each action).

    Key Contributions¶

    • Twelve custom UE5 games spanning Solo/PvP/Coop with unified action interfaces, achieving 0.0% pre-training recognition rate.
    • The Improvement Dynamics Curve (IDC): a multi-round agentic-reflection harness producing a full score trajectory and a held-out transfer score per (agent, game) pair.
    • An autonomous tool-using reflector with a fixed small tool surface (read, diagnose, judge, write) rather than a fixed-template script.
    • An empirical study across twelve agents (commercial VLMs, open-weight VLMs, specialized policies) showing that game leadership rotates and that origin-task improvement diverges from transfer performance.

    Results¶

    • No single model dominates Solo: GPT-5.5 leads 4 of 7 Solo games; Claude Opus 4.6 wins CueChase (0.840 vs. next 0.580); Gemini 3.1 Pro leads MonsterShoot (0.710 vs. next 0.464).
    • Newer ≠ better: Claude Opus 4.6 outperforms Opus 4.7 on 5 of 7 Solo games; GPT-5.4 exceeds GPT-5.5 on SceneEscape.
    • Commercial vs. open-weight gap: Both Qwen3.5 MoE checkpoints score 0.000 on both Coop games; specialized policies (NitroGen, Open-P2P) also fail to transfer to most games.
    • Coop ceiling: GPT-5.5 leads both Coop games but reaches only 0.368 (SharedFloor) and 0.184 (HandoffRun), indicating LLM-LLM coordination is far from solved.
    • IDC: All four top agents tested under IDC improve over their cold-start baseline through reflection, but peak performance is typically reached mid-curve rather than at the final round.
    • Transfer divergence: Origin-task improvement and held-out variant transfer scores diverge across agents, a pattern hidden by single-round leaderboard scores.

    Limitations¶

    • IDC is reported for only 4 of the 12 evaluated agents, so the full picture of reflection dynamics across the agent pool is incomplete.
    • Mechanics leakage remains at 50.0% even for OmniGameArena (though lower than competitors), meaning partial rule recall cannot be ruled out.
    • The reflector and judge are themselves LLMs whose quality affects IDC outcomes; reflector model choice is not ablated.
    • All VLMs use the chunked keyboard-mouse adapter rather than native real-time control, potentially disadvantaging agents tuned for faster action loops.
    • PvP evaluation covers agent-vs.-agent pairings within the same pool; human-level opponents are not included.
    • \(K\) (episodes per round) and \(R\) (rounds) values are not specified in the excerpted text, limiting reproducibility assessment.

    Relevance to Vision-Language Models¶

    OmniGameArena directly benchmarks VLMs as embodied, real-time decision-makers in visually complex 3D environments, probing capabilities — spatial navigation, reaction timing, adversarial reasoning, cooperative coordination — that static Q&A and captioning benchmarks cannot reveal. The contamination-controlled game suite fills a gap left by prior benchmarks that reuse commercial titles, enabling cleaner measurement of VLM visual generalization. The IDC harness operationalizes the question of whether VLMs can self-improve through language-based reflection without weight updates, which is central to the emerging paradigm of prompt-level continual learning. The finding that origin-task improvement does not predict transfer is a direct signal for researchers studying the limits of in-context skill acquisition in VLMs.
