    Delayed Verification Destabilizes Multi-Agent LLM Belief: Instability Thresholds and Optimal Corrector Placement¶

    🕒 Published (v1): 2026-06-25 00:00 UTC · Source: HuggingFace · link

    TL;DR¶

    Delayed verification in multi-agent LLM systems is modeled as a delayed consensus on a graph with grounded corrector nodes. Spectral decomposition of the grounded Laplacian yields a closed-form stability threshold: verification that is too strong or too delayed turns consensus into oscillation, with the inverse golden ratio (\((\sqrt{5} - 1)/2 \approx 0.618\)) as the critical dose at delay two. A greedy \((1-1/e)\)-approximation rule places limited corrector budgets on the network's amplifier and bridge nodes.

    Problem¶

    Multi-agent LLM systems use verifier/critic agents to suppress hallucinations, but verification has latency — a verifier reads a claim, retrieves evidence, and returns a correction after several interaction steps, during which the unverified claim has already propagated. The current literature offers no dynamical account of whether the very act of delayed verification can destabilize the factuality it is meant to protect, nor does it supply a closed-form trade-off between verification dose (strength), delay, and corrector placement on the network graph.

    Method¶

    The paper casts the corrected multi-agent loop as a discrete-time delayed consensus on a graph \(G = (V, E)\) with grounded corrector nodes \(R \subseteq V\) that hold at truth (\(e_{i,t}=0, i \in R\)). Free agents carry scalar error \(e_t \in \mathbb{R}^{n_f}\) and evolve as:

    \[e_{t+1} = (I - \eta L_g) e_t - \eta \kappa \, e_{t-\delta} + \eta g\]

    where \(L_g\) is the grounded Laplacian (principal submatrix on free nodes), \(\eta\) the consensus step size, \(\kappa\) the verification gain, \(\delta\) the integer verification latency, and \(g\) the faulty-agent forcing. Lemma 1 shows that because \(\kappa I\) commutes with \(L_g\), the \(n_f\)-dimensional system decouples into independent scalar recurrences via the eigenbasis of \(L_g\) — one recurrence per eigenvalue \(\mu_i\) of \(L_g\):

    \[x_{i,t+1} = (1 - \eta\mu_i) x_{i,t} - \eta\kappa \, x_{i,t-\delta} + \eta\hat{g}_i\]

    Each scalar mode is analyzed via its characteristic polynomial \(z^{\delta+1} - a_i z^\delta + \beta = 0\) (\(\beta = \eta\kappa\)). The oscillatory boundary occurs when a complex conjugate pair \(e^{\pm i\theta}\) crosses the unit circle, yielding the closed-form critical dose:

    \[\beta_c = \frac{\sin\theta}{\sin(\delta\theta)}, \quad a = \frac{\sin((\delta+1)\theta)}{\sin(\delta\theta)}, \quad \theta \in (0, \pi/\delta)\]

    Using Chebyshev polynomials of the second kind, \(\beta_c\) is shown to be strictly decreasing in both \(a\) and \(\delta\) (Lemma 2), so the binding mode is always the slowest grounded mode (smallest \(\mu_i\)), and longer delay forces a strictly smaller safe verification strength.

    For corrector placement under a budget \(k\), steady-state error is \(e_\infty(R) = (L_g + \kappa I)^{-1}g\). Averaging over unknown fault locations gives coherence \(H(R) = \operatorname{tr}\, M(R)^{-1}\), which is supermodular — its reduction \(\rho(R)\) is submodular, so a one-pass greedy rule (Algorithm 1: pin the node with largest resolvent centrality \(\Delta_i(R) = w\|M(R)^{-1}e_i\|^2 / (1 + w e_i^\top M(R)^{-1}e_i)\)) achieves a \((1-1/e)\) approximation guarantee (Theorem 2).

    Two coupled delays (gossip latency \(d\) + verification latency \(\delta\)) are handled via \(D\)-decomposition, yielding a trinomial stability region whose worst case is the synchronized-delay corner (\(d=\delta\)), where the ceiling collapses to \(\beta_c(1,\delta)\) — the same inverse golden ratio sup at \(\delta=2\).

    Empirical validation spans five open models (Qwen3.6-35B, Qwen3-14B, Mistral-7B, Phi-4, Gemma-4-12B) on both synthetic saturating systems and real multi-agent numeric-estimation debates.

    Key Contributions¶

    • A model of the delayed-verification multi-agent LLM loop as delayed consensus with grounded correctors, decoupled into independent scalar delay recurrences via the grounded Laplacian (Lemma 1).
    • A closed-form stability threshold \(\beta_c(a,\delta)\) for the verification dose, monotone decreasing in both \(a\) and \(\delta\), with the inverse golden ratio \(1/\varphi \approx 0.618\) as the critical dose at \(\delta=2\) (Theorem 1, Proposition 2, Lemma 2, Corollary 1).
    • A greedy corrector-placement algorithm with a \((1-1/e)\)-optimality guarantee via supermodular coherence over the grounded-Laplacian resolvent (Theorem 2).
    • Two-delay stability analysis showing synchronized gossip and verification delays are the least stable configuration (Theorem 3, Corollary 3).
    • Empirical confirmation across five open models that signed dose–delay oscillations occur at the predicted threshold, while grounded factual answering remains convergent (Remark 1).

    Results¶

    • Synthetic onset (RQ1): On a random grounded graph (\(n_f=8\)), the nonlinear saturating system (\(\tanh\) verifier) oscillates at \(\kappa_{\text{crit}}\) within \(\sim 1.7\%\) of the predicted \(\kappa_{\max}\) across \(\delta = 1,2,3\) (e.g., \(\kappa_{\max}=10.21\) vs. \(\kappa_{\text{crit}}=10.38\) at \(\delta=2\)); the \(\delta=2\) period (measured 9.38 rounds) matches \(2\pi/\theta^* = 9.72\).
    • Grounded factual debate (RQ2): A grounded verifier of moderate strength drives the loop to convergence (NLI score \(\approx 0.8\)); an over-aggressive verifier prevents convergence (\(\approx 0.4\)); an ungrounded contrarian critic destroys it entirely (91% of rounds flip the majority answer). Across 180 TriviaQA/PsiloQA debates, consensus-error amplitude does not grow with \(\delta\) for grounded factual QA — the delay-induced oscillation is confined to the signed-belief regime.
    • Signed oscillation across models (RQ3, Table 1): Five models show 96–100% of debates overshoot through zero (the Hopf signature) at unstable settings (\(\alpha=0.5\), \(\delta=6\)) vs. 0–4% at stable settings (\(\delta=1\)); all reach 8/8 question-level unanimity (one-sided Wilcoxon \(p=0.004\), survives Bonferroni).

    Limitations¶

    • The analysis assumes a symmetric Laplacian (reciprocal influence in peer debate or blackboard memory); strictly directed pipelines (generator \(\to\) verifier \(\to\) rewriter) are non-normal and fall outside scope.
    • Belief is modeled as a single scalar; content-level phenomena (truth dilution, claim provenance, epistemic laundering) are out of scope by construction.
    • The corrector is oracle-but-delayed; verifier error is not modeled — the destabilizing ingredient is delay alone.
    • The supermodular structure and \((1-1/e)\) guarantee for corrector placement are inherited from linear leader-selection theory and assume the linear diffusion model; carrying them to fully nonlinear error-propagation maps is an open assumption.
    • The empirical signed-belief study uses 8 author-curated quantity questions and a single wrong-majority setting — not a large-scale benchmark.

    Relevance to Agentic AI / LLM Agents¶

    This paper provides the first dynamical stability analysis of the verification loop that is the backbone of multi-agent LLM reliability. It gives a concrete, closed-form design rule (the dose–delay boundary) for tuning verifier strength against latency, and a principled budgeted placement strategy for corrector agents on a graph. The core finding — that delayed negative feedback can oscillate rather than converge — is directly applicable to any multi-agent architecture where critic/verifier agents run with non-negligible latency (e.g., retrieval-augmented verification, tool-use-heavy fact-checking loops). The empirical dichotomy between grounded factual (stable) and signed-belief (oscillatory) regimes also sharpens the understanding of when multi-agent debate degenerates, complementing prior empirical observations of accuracy loss over rounds.
